Chapter One: The Grey Pyramid

Elyndra the Vaadagh stumbled from the limestone maw of Ochie Hole with the taste of ozone and old blood still coating her tongue. The sceptre’s prismatic blow struck her like a cursed meteor - rainbow fire that burned without heat, flinging her forward through the tearing planes while Kael Draven’s final roar echoed behind her, crystallising into silence. She remembered the Witch’s weird teeth - far too many, the wet laugh, the way Kael’s scarred body had stiffened vein by vein into glittering quartz, axe.. half-raised, eyes still alive for three terrible heartbeats. Then the blast. Then nothing but timesickness clawing at the edges of her mind, memories disintegrating rapidly, her identity pared back layer by layer until only instinct and ache remained.

She stood in a field of wet grass that smelled of crushed nettles and distant burning oil. Glastonbury Tor rose before her, its green spine crowned by the squat tower she recognised from alien scrolls as a thin, liminal place, a wound in the world. The sky above was wrong - too blue, too ordinary - yet threaded with the faint violet rifts of the Second Conjunction already beginning to yawn. Azarok’s laughter, or the Laughing God as some called the trickster in this fractured age, threaded the wind like distant bells made of broken glass. Soon, it promised. Blood and chaos and the sweet ruin of desire.

Five and a half miles of mud and fracturing memory lay between her and the strange pyramid squatting at the heart of the gathering like a misplaced altar to some mechanical deity. Elyndra’s silver robes - once the colour of starlight on Elmiboran snow, edged with bronze filigree worked in the great-age manner of the Vaadagh smiths who had forged empires before the planes themselves grew weary - clung damply to her legs. The hem, heavy with embroidered sigils of ley-lines and the multiverse’s hidden roads, dragged through dew. The black-iron collar Kael had locked about her throat in the barrow still rested cool and alive against her collarbones; it pulsed once, a faint echo of the Thirst Eternal that had braided their life-forces in molten surrender, then fell still. She touched it and the memory slipped away like blood down a drain, leaving only the ache and the vague imperative: Save him. Find the cave. The Witch waits.

Timesickness chewed at her mind as she began the descent. Each step unravelled another thread. She remembered the Tower of Eternal Dominion, the crystal strongholds of her people, the way Vaadagh philosophers had contemplated the end of all things in perfect melancholy while the careless Mabden herds bellowed and bred in the mud below. She remembered Kael’s storm-grey eyes above her, his scarred Denryssii hands pinning her wrists while the collar drank and gave back life in equal measure. She remembered the Witch’s sceptre thrusting forward, flesh crawling into quartz. Then the memories frayed. By the time she reached the base of the tor, she knew only that she was lost, that the planes were dying, and that something precious - someone - lay petrified behind her in darkness.

Horseless wagons - great iron beasts of every hue, squat and growling or sleek as hunting cats - rumbled past on black roads that cut the green like scars. Their eyes glowed red or white; their breath stank of burned offerings to some crude Azarok of infernal devices. Elyndra dodged between them with the grace of one who had once danced the measured pavanes of the Tower, yet each evasion cost her another fragment of herself. A flash: silk restraints biting her wrists, Kael’s mouth at her throat while the collar flared and their essences braided. Gone. Only the mud between her bare toes and the distant throb of strange music remained.

The pyramid grew larger as she walked, a sharp ziggurat of steel and canvas crowned with lights that twinkled like captured stars. Around it stretched a vast grey metallic wall, seamless and cold, encircling fields turned into a labyrinth of tents and fires. The air hummed with the low laughter of the Laughing God; she heard it in the rumbling that rolled across the grass like distant thunder. Soon.

She reached the wall at last, breathless, the hem of her robe torn and streaked with the earth of this crude carnival. Two figures lounged against the barrier, their silhouettes ragged against the floodlights. Crustie travellers, the other Mabden called them - though Elyndra’s fading mind supplied no such name. The woman wore a leather jacket patched with faded cotton cloth and armoured with metal studs, sleeves slashed to reveal arms inked with crude sigils of rebellion; beneath it, a threadbare vest clung to small, high breasts. Her hair was a riot of green dreadlocks threaded with feathers and bones, her face pierced with silver rings that caught the light like ritual piercings of the Denryssii shamans. The man beside her sported a kilt of torn tartan over heavy boots, his bare chest crossed with bandoliers of wire pins and tin disks; a faded thick cotton vest hung open, revealing a torso mapped with scars and warlike tattoos of grinning skulls. They smelled of myrrh, sweat, and the sweet rot of cheap wine - vital, fertile, low, and yet the old Vaadagh disdain curled in her belly alongside something warmer, hungrier.

“Oi, love,” the woman drawled in a dialect that twisted like a half-remembered incantation. “You look proper lost. Wall’s higher every year, innit? Festival proper Friggasday. Today’s Tyrsday, tomorrow – Wotansday - is the solstice, mind. Snide parties inside and out. But you can’t just stroll through without the toll.”

Elyndra blinked. The words slid across her mind like oil on water. “Toll?”

“A full gold sovereign,” the man said, grinning with teeth stained by whatever herb they smoked. “Or climb the bastard - though the razor-wire’ll carve you pretty. Or…” His eyes raked her robes, lingering on the bronze embroidery that spoke of empires older than this realm. “Or have a moment with one of the guards at the gate. They’re bored, like. Friendly. Just don't call them freekwid, they don't like reminding.”

The woman laughed, low and rough. “Don’t mind him. He’s half-cut already. But yeah, that’s the price. Take it or leave it, star-hair.”

Star-hair. The endearment pierced her like a spear-point. Kael had called her something like that once, before the collar had claimed them both. The memory flared - his breath hot against her ear as the collar pulsed mine, mine, ours - then guttered out. She swayed.

“I… have no coin of this realm,” she said, voice soft as velvet soaked in old blood. The words felt archaic even to her; the pair giggled as though she had told the finest jest.

“Realm?” the woman echoed, delighted. “Fuckin’ hell, you’re deep in character already. Come on, then. Camp with us tonight. Name’s Cáit - mum's Irish, innit? This is Toad. We’ll sort you a spot inside the outer fields. Tomorrow’s solstice; parties everywhere. You’ll fit right in with that get-up. Looks proper medieval, that robe. Turkish threads? Sick.”

They helped her over a low section of fencing where the metal gave way to wire, their hands rough but not unkind - calloused Mabden hands that steadied her as though she were some fragile relic. Elyndra let them. Contempt curled in her belly, the old Vaadagh disdain for the braying herds, yet beneath it stirred something warmer, hungrier. The woman - Cáit - smelled of smoke and warm flesh, and her eyes held a spark that reminded Elyndra of the way Kael had looked at her when the Tenth Ritual first flared.

They led her toward the outer encampments which lay scattered and low, small clusters of tents and battered vans half-hidden in the hedgerows and ditches. Fires crackled softly between them, and music pulsed from battered black boxes  -  voices of iron and thunder rolling across the dark fields.  Small groups danced in crude, wild ecstasy, bodies painted with mud and glowing paint, hair tangled, clothes a riot of patches, leather, and ripped denim. Their savage joy echoed the barbarian finery of the Denryssii, yet carried none of their cold grandeur.  Elyndra’s gaze lingered on a girl in a cropped vest and torn skirts, hips swaying fiercely to the war-drum rhythm. Something deep inside her stirred. The collar at her throat pulsed once, hot and sharp.

They found a firepit ringed by a small circle of similar wanderers. Cáit produced a blanket woven of rough wool and spread it beside her own. “Sleep here tonight. Festival proper starts Friggasday, but tonight we rest for the solstice dawn. Tomorrow  the real madness starts. Parties inside the gates, outside too. You’ll see.”

Elyndra sat, legs folding beneath her in the manner of her tower’s philosophers. The firelight painted her silver hair with copper, turned the bronze edging of her robes to molten gold. She tried to speak of herself - of the ley-lines she had mapped across intersecting planes, of the Tower of Eternal Dominion where Vaadagh scholars once contemplated the end of all things in perfect melancholy. But the words twisted. “I am… cartographer of… the hidden roads between…” A giggle rippled around the circle. “Fucking hell, she’s proper method acting,” someone muttered. Another burst of laughter. The more she tried to remember, the more the timesickness chewed at the edges, leaving only fragments: Kael’s blade-kissed hand pinning her wrists, the collar drinking their joined essences; the Witch’s wet laugh; the imperative to save him.

Music swelled from a nearby box - great metallic clanging, repetitive as a ritual chant, layered over screams like souls in the Conjunction’s maw. The words drifted across the fire:

“Laughing in the dark, the god that never sleeps,  

Raze the veil, drink the blood, let the chaos creep…  

Corax calls,

the black wings spread,  

All your empires fall, all your pretty dead…”

The lyric struck her like a spell spoken in the high tongue of Elmibor. She sat upright, heart hammering. The rhythm was crude, the voice a low growl that carried the same sinister cadence as Azarok’s own mirth. Raze Corax, they called him - Corax to those who feared or worshipped. Some loved him, some spat at his name. A musician of this plane, yet the song tasted of the Laughing God’s promise: blood and chaos and the sweet ruin of desire. Her memory flickered - Kael’s face, then another, sharper, shadowed by stage-lights and the same storm-grey eyes. Different aspects of the same master? The thought dissolved before she could seize it.

Cáit noticed her tension. “You all right, love? Corax, yeah? Proper Marmite bastard. Some say he’s a genius, others reckon he’s proper evil - sacrifices groupies or some bollocks. Song’s a banger though.”

Elyndra could only nod. The night deepened. Conversations washed over her; she smiled when they laughed, offered fragments that earned more giggles. The fire burned low. One by one the others drifted to their tents until only Cáit remained, her green dreads loose about her shoulders, eyes bright with wine and something sharper.


“You’re not from round here,” Cáit said quietly, no mockery now. “Not really. That look in your eyes - like you’ve seen worlds fall and still can’t look away. Come here.”

It was not a command, yet it pulled at the place inside Elyndra where the collar’s echo still lived. She moved, silk and bronze whispering against wool. Their mouths met - tentative at first, then hungry. Cáit tasted of smoke and salt and the raw fertility of the Mabden herd. Elyndra’s hands slid beneath the leather jacket, finding warm skin marked by ink and piercings. She pushed the smaller woman back onto the blanket, robes pooling around them like spilled starlight on a dying sea.

The power exchange ignited like a ley-line flaring at the Conjunction’s edge. Elyndra rose above her, silver hair cascading like frozen moonlight, the bronze-edged robes parting to bare the pale, luminous skin of a Vaadagh scholar who had once mapped the multiverse’s final breaths. Her fingers - long, elegant, callused only from the stylus of forgotten charts - circled Cáit’s throat in a slow, deliberate pressure, light enough to promise safety, firm enough to remind the Mabden woman that empires older than her nation once ruled the stars. The black-iron collar at Elyndra’s own throat pulsed once, warm as living blood, drinking the faint life-force that rose between them and feeding it back in a thin, exquisite thread.

“Feel it,” Elyndra whispered, voice like velvet soaked in the blood of dying suns. “The weight of a thousand fallen planes upon your pulse. You are the herd, crude and fertile and alive beneath me… yet tonight you tremble for a decadent fiend who should despise you.” Her thumb brushed the silver ring piercing Cáit’s lower lip, then pressed gently against the artery that fluttered like a trapped moth.

Cáit’s eyes widened, pupils blown wide with defiant want. A low, guttural sound escaped her - half laugh, half plea - as her hips arched upward, seeking friction against Elyndra’s thigh. “Fuck… yes. Harder - soft, but harder. Make me feel it, star-fucker.”

Elyndra obliged with the ritual precision of one who had once bound lovers in the Tower’s silk chambers. Beneath her, Cáit bucked, hands fisting in the silver strands of Elyndra’s hair, but Elyndra held dominion, thighs pinning the Mabden woman’s hips as her own robes fell open like the petals of some ancient, forbidden flower. Pale skin met tattooed warmth; the collar flared again, sending a ripple of shared life-force through them both - Elyndra’s melancholy cosmic hunger meeting Cáit’s raw, roaring vitality. Philosophical fragments spilled between kisses, each word laced with the despair of ending worlds. “All planes end in laughter,” Elyndra murmured against the curve of a breast, voice husky with need. “All desire is merely Azarok’s jest before the final veil tears. Yet here, in the mud of your heathen festival, I would drink your surrender as though it could stay the Conjunction itself.”

Cáit moaned, the sound raw and unashamed, her body surrendering even as her eyes flashed defiance. “Then take it… take what the herd can give you.” Her legs parted wider, inviting, and Elyndra slid two fingers deep into slick heat, curling them with deliberate mastery while her thumb circled the swollen pearl above. The choking remained light, safe - Elyndra’s palm a living restraint that read every flutter of pulse like a ley-line chart of the multiverse - yet it bound them in ritual dominance: trembling surrender on one side, obsessive control on the other. Hips rolled in slow, deliberate rhythm; sweat-slick skin slid together with obscene, beautiful friction. Cáit’s nails raked down Elyndra’s back, leaving faint red trails that the collar drank and healed in the same breath.

Climax built like the slow approach of a dying star - Cáit first, body arching in defiant ecstasy, inner walls clenching around Elyndra’s fingers as she cried out into the night. The sound carried the raw heat of the herds and the cosmic melancholy of lost empires. Elyndra followed moments later, riding the aftershocks with a low, velvet moan that tasted of Valhalla and blood, her own release a trembling confession pressed against Cáit’s throat.

Afterward they lay tangled, sweat cooling on skin that still hummed with shared life-force. Cáit’s head rested on Elyndra’s breast, green dreadlocks spilling across pale Vaadagh flesh like wild vines claiming an ancient ruin. Tenderness bloomed, unexpected and aching: Elyndra’s fingers stroked through the dreads with a gentleness that belied the dominance of moments before, tracing the line of a tattoo as though mapping a dying constellation. “Even in this wanton age,” she whispered, “you carry a beauty the philosophers of my Tower never named. A spark that might yet outlast the planes’ final sigh.”

Yet the shadow lingered. In the half-dream before sleep, Azarok’s laughter echoed through caverns of her mind, demanding chaos and blood. Kael’s face - scarred, storm-eyed - flickered beside another: Corax, perhaps, or the same soul wearing a different mask. The pyramid waited beyond the wall, silent promise of answers or further ruin. Her lover Kael Draven lay petrified somewhere in a cave of crystal, waiting for her to discover how to save him. Or perhaps to become something new in the process.

The solstice loomed. Tomorrow the thresholds would open wider, the Mabden hordes would roar, and celestial music would call across the fractured planes. Elyndra closed her eyes, the taste of Cáit still on her tongue, the ache of unresolved passion coiled low in her belly like the first pulse of the Thirst Eternal reborn in mud and astral shimmer.

The planes themselves might soon end. But tonight, in the festival’s careless embrace, desire had not yet chosen between salvation and ruin.

Chapter Two: The Veil of the Laughing Dawn

Elyndra the Vaadagh woke to a sky the colour of dull bruises and breaking waves, the black-iron collar at her throat a cool, living weight that pulsed once like the last heartbeat of a lost nephilim. Dew lay heavy upon the rough wool beneath her, cold as the snows that once mantled the crystal spires of the Tower of Eternal Dominion, and the air tasted of crushed grass and woodsmoke. The metallic promise of the Second Conjunction already tearing violet wounds across the dawn. Timesickness gnawed at the marrow of her mind, peeling memories as a philistine might strip vellum from an ancient codex in the final days of Elmibor: the melancholy isolation of Vaadagh philosopher-artists contemplating the slow death of all planes within their fortress-mansions; Kael Draven’s battle-scored Denryssii hands pinning her wrists while the Thirst Eternal braided their life-forces in molten ritual surrender; the Witch’s sceptre flaring rainbow fire, flesh crystallising vein by vein into quartz while Azarok’s laughter rang like shattered bells through the rifts. All of it frayed at the edges, leaving only ache and the iron imperative that burned low in her belly like a coal in the heart of a dying sun: find the key! Save him! The planes themselves are laughing their last...

A calloused hand shook her shoulder, warm and alive with the savage, carnal vitality of the Mabden breed. Cáit crouched close, green dreadlocks threaded with feathers and little bones swinging like the talismans of some spaewife or hellerune. Her patched leather jacket - sleeves slashed in the manner of Iron Age war-harness to bare inked arms marked with coarse runes of defiance - hung open over a threadbare vest that clung damply to the small, delicious curves of her breasts, the fabric worn thin as ancient battle-linen. afoot betwixt the star-lit sage and hedge-born traveller, between the cold grandeur of dying empires and the vital throb of living flesh.

Corax. The name slid across Elyndra’s tongue like the echo of Azarok’s own mirth - sweet, poisonous, inevitable. They moved through the outer encampments like ghosts between planes. Grass wet and cold beneath Elyndra’s bare feet; the air heavy with myrrh, spilled ale, and the reek of unwashed bodies. A huge, low stone stood at its heart, crowned by a bonfire whose flames licked upward seeking the violet rifts above. Already the press of bodies had gathered - skin painted with mud and glowing symbols, cloaks of faded velvet and patched denim swirling like the ritual robes of some Bronze Age tribe long forgotten. Horned headdresses rose above the throng; bare skin gleamed with sweat and dew. The air hummed with low chanting and the thunder of drums that pulsed like the heartbeat of the Conjunction itself.

Elyndra and Cáit slipped into the tide just as the first pale blade of sunrise cut the horizon. And there, upon the tallest standing stone, stood Raze Corax.

Power rolled from him in slow, decadent waves - raw, chaotic, drenched in the laughter of Azarok. Decadent leech , the old Vaadagh sneer whispered. Yet the planes fracture, and you stand crowned in their ruin.
